---
title: Ethics in the AI Age
author: C. James
date: 2026-04-14T12:00:00Z
tags: [philosophy, ai, ethics, obligation, deconstruction]
---

I have been for some time now coming back to a question in the AI space as an engineer. This concept of ethics of AI. I stand here ready to confess my position and suffer any consequences from it. In the words of Caputo, I am against ethics. In this day and age where there are philosophers hired to create an ethics for LLMs, I am here to say that this ethics where obligation "which makes ethics blush" is not safe. Ethics in the philosophical sense at its root has been a work to honor obligation, to make it safe. Like Caputo, I think when we enter into conversation around an ethics in regard to LLMs that I want to begin the work of liberating obligation from ethics.

What is called AI ethics today often arrives as governance theater: principles, checklists, and policy decks that promise responsibility while leaving the machine of production untouched. We are invited to believe that if we tune fairness metrics, draft model cards, and add a review board, we have addressed the ethical question. But this move repeats what Caputo diagnoses in Against Ethics: ethics can become a shelter from the force of obligation, a way to domesticate what should unsettle us.

If we ask, with Derrida, what makes a decision just, we are forced into a more difficult space. In Force of Law he argues that a responsible decision cannot simply apply a rule; it must pass through undecidability. The present discourse around LLMs seeks the opposite: remove undecidability, formalize every risk, automate accountability. Yet obligation does not arrive as a clean variable in a safety pipeline. It appears as interruption, as demand, often from those who are least represented in the training corpus and least protected by the institutions deploying the systems.

Levinas helps sharpen this. Obligation is not first a contract among equals; it is an asymmetrical claim made by the Other before I choose it (Totality and Infinity; Otherwise than Being). In AI work this means the most important demand may come from those who do not benefit from scale: workers labeling toxic data under precarious conditions, communities rendered legible only as risk profiles, students and patients translated into prediction targets. If our ethics begins only after product strategy, then we have already betrayed obligation.

Zizek adds another warning: ideology does not disappear when we become critical of ideology. It returns as cynical distance, where we know very well what we are doing, yet we do it anyway. AI ethics can function precisely this way. We declare transparency while building opacity, declare inclusion while optimizing extraction, declare human-centered design while replacing human judgment with confidence scores. The problem is not hypocrisy in a moralistic sense; the problem is that ethics language itself can become the mechanism by which antagonism is managed and neutralized.

So a deconstruction of AI ethics does not mean abandoning responsibility. It means refusing the comforting fiction that ethics is a stable object we can install into a model lifecycle. The question shifts from What is the right framework? to To whom are we obligated before any framework begins? Obligation precedes architecture, and it exceeds compliance. It asks what we owe to those our systems can harm, erase, or silently reorder.

Practically, this would change engineering habits: not only benchmark performance, but map sacrifice; not only assess bias, but identify who absorbs system failure; not only red-team prompts, but audit labor chains and institutional incentives. It would require stopping some deployments not because they violate a published principle, but because they cannot be reconciled with the claims of those made vulnerable by them. In short, obligation is not a module in AI ethics. Obligation is what judges AI ethics.

References: John D. Caputo, Against Ethics (1993). Jacques Derrida, Force of Law: The Mystical Foundation of Authority (1990). Emmanuel Levinas, Totality and Infinity (1961) and Otherwise than Being (1974). Slavoj Zizek, The Sublime Object of Ideology (1989).
