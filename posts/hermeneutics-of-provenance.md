---
title: The Hermeneutics of Provenance
author: C. James
date: 2026-06-29T12:00:00Z
tags: [philosophy, security, provenance, supply-chain, derrida, lacan, badiou, levinas, zizek]

There is a strange piety in the way the software industry treats provenance. We collect signatures, we publish SBOMs, we cryptographically attest. And then, having performed these rites, we file them. Provenance is treated as a kind of birth certificate: issued once, archived, occasionally produced at the border. The artifact has its papers. The papers settle the matter. I want to argue against this, and to argue against it not from a technical standpoint, where the argument has already been made many times, but from a hermeneutic one. Provenance is not a non living text. It is a living text. It demands a hermeneutics, and that hermeneutics has to be loyal to a particular feature of its object: that what it interprets is never finished.

Begin with the most obvious case. A maintainer publishes a release. Today they have never been phished. Their hardware key is in their pocket. Their commits are signed. Every standard of provenance is met. Tomorrow they are phished. The attacker resets their email, gains access to their registry token, and publishes nothing — not a single new release. The compromise leaves no artifact behind. And yet the question of what we should now think of yesterday's release is not closed. The cryptographic facts have not changed. The hermeneutic facts have. The trust we extended was extended on the basis of an inference about an agent. That inference is now revised. Not erased, revised. The text that is the artifact has acquired a new reading.

This is what I want to call the hermeneutics of provenance. And I want to develop it by way of five thinkers who, on the face of it, have nothing whatever to say about software supply chains, but who have everything to say about how meaning gets fixed, unfixed, and refixed by events that arrive late.

The first is Derrida. The model of provenance we have inherited is signature-driven, and Derrida's "Signature Event Context" is precisely the dismantling of the signature as a guarantor of presence. The signature pretends to bind a unique event of intention to a mark on a page. But the very feature that makes the signature recognizable — its iterability — also makes it severable from the original act of signing. A signature only works if it can be repeated; and if it can be repeated, then it can be repeated in the absence, the incapacity, or the compromise of the one who first inscribed it. The Sigstore certificate, the GPG key, the hardware token: each one is a writing that survives its author, and that survival is not a bug but the condition of its functioning. Derrida calls the structure of this surviving mark the trace. The trace is never a presence; it is always already a deferral, a différance, of the presence it claims to bear witness to. To read provenance is to read traces, never to verify presences. Anyone who has run a key rotation already knows this in their hands. The hermeneutician knows it as a thesis.

If Derrida gives us the structure of the mark, Lacan gives us the structure of the time in which the mark is read. The crucial Lacanian concept here is nachträglichkeit, après-coup, retroactive constitution. Meaning, for Lacan, is never simply present in the moment of utterance; it is fixed by what comes later, by the punctuation of the sentence, by the analysand's slip three sessions on, by the symbolic event that retroactively reorganizes the chain of signifiers that led to it. We are familiar with this in trauma, where an event in the past becomes traumatic only after a later event has named it as such. The compromised maintainer is a perfect figure of après-coup. The phishing tomorrow does not change yesterday's commit as a sequence of bytes. It changes yesterday's commit as a signifier within a chain that now terminates in the master signifier compromised. The hash is stable. The meaning of the hash is not. This is why a hermeneutics of provenance cannot be archival in the simple sense. The archive can only hold what has already happened. The meaning of what has already happened is hostage to what has not yet happened.

This is also why the dominant industry stance toward provenance is, in the strict Lacanian sense, fetishistic. Zizek's reading of the fetish is the relevant one: I know very well, but all the same. I know very well that any key can be exfiltrated, that any maintainer can be coerced, that any build farm can be backdoored, but all the same I treat the attestation as if it were a closed proof. The SBOM functions ideologically not because it is false — it is, on its own terms, true — but because it occupies the place where a sustained interpretive practice ought to be, and by occupying that place it relieves us of the practice. Zizek would point out that the more elaborate the attestation framework becomes, the more efficiently it can serve this disavowing function. The danger is not bad provenance. The danger is excellent provenance that has been promoted from evidence to verdict.

To break the fetish we need a category of the event that is stronger than the category of the fact, and here Badiou is indispensable. For Badiou, an event is not just one more thing that has happened. An event is a rupture in the situation that compels a reorganization of what counts in that situation. The phishing of a maintainer, the disclosure of a backdoor in a build tool, the revelation that a popular library was for two years controlled by a state actor — these are not data points to be ingested into a risk dashboard. They are events in the strong sense. They demand what Badiou calls fidelity: the ongoing labor of working out what the situation now means in light of the event, of identifying which prior commitments survive the event and which were sustained only by the situation the event has interrupted. The xz-utils episode of 2024 was a Badiouian event for the open source world, and the fidelity it demanded was not merely "patch this CVE." It was a reinterrogation of every long maintainer-handover, every solo-maintained foundational utility, every "trusted because old" inference. Most of that interrogation has not happened. A hermeneutics of provenance is, on this register, a discipline of fidelity to events that have already broken the situation and to events that will.

But fidelity to whom? Here is where Levinas must enter, because without him the hermeneutics of provenance threatens to become merely a more sophisticated form of self-protection by the consumer of the software. Levinas reorients the entire question of trust away from the calculative posture of the I who must defend itself against deception, and toward the asymmetrical claim of the Other. The maintainer is not first a risk surface. They are a face. The obligation runs in both directions and it runs before any verification. We owe them the presumption of good faith that any human agent is owed, and we owe them the recognition that their compromise — if it ever comes — was a wound done to them before it was a wound done to us. We also owe something to the third party Levinas calls le tiers: the downstream user who will absorb the failure of our interpretive laziness. A hermeneutics of provenance that is only paranoid is not yet ethical. It only watches its own back. A hermeneutics of provenance that is Levinasian recognizes that to interpret the trace of another human's labor is already to stand in a relation of responsibility, both to that human and to those whom our reading of their work will affect. The cryptographic verification is downstream of this responsibility, not the source of it.

What, then, does the practice actually look like? I will resist the temptation to turn this into a checklist, because the whole argument has been that checklists are how the practice dies. But some shape can be given.

First, provenance must be read as a text that has tenses. Past attestations describe a past agent. The agent persists. The persistence of the agent is not attested by anything in the past attestation. Any reading of provenance that does not have a present-tense and future-tense dimension is incomplete. The trust statement "this artifact was signed by Alice in 2024" is not equivalent to the trust statement "this artifact is to be trusted in 2026," and conflating them is the first ideological move that a hermeneutics of provenance has to refuse.

Second, the disclosure of a compromise must be treated as a Badiouian event with retroactive reach, not as a point fix. When a maintainer announces that their key was exfiltrated, the question is not only "which artifacts signed after the compromise are tainted" but "which of our interpretive habits were sustained by the assumption that this maintainer was, as we put it, in the clear." The latter question is harder, less tractable, and more important. It is the question fidelity asks.

Third, the relation to the maintainer must be carried as a relation, in the Levinasian sense, and not collapsed into a transaction. This has uncomfortable practical implications. It means that the ecosystems that treat solo maintainers of critical infrastructure as untipped service workers cannot be reconciled with any serious hermeneutics of provenance, because they have already deformed the relation in which interpretation would have to take place. You cannot have a healthy hermeneutics of provenance on top of a sick relation to the human source of the text.

Fourth, the fetish must be named. Every time an organization treats the existence of an SBOM, an SLSA level, or a signed attestation as ending the conversation, the fetish is operating. The Zizekian move is not to abolish the artifact — the SBOM is useful — but to refuse the disavowal it enables. The artifact is evidence. It is not verdict.

Fifth and last, the trace must be read as trace, in Derrida's sense. The signature does not deliver the signer. It defers them. The deferral is structural, not a defect to be engineered around. A mature security posture would internalize this rather than promise its opposite with each new attestation standard.

I want to close on what may be the most demanding consequence of this view. If provenance is a living text, then no release is ever finally trusted, and no release is ever finally untrusted. Trust is a verb conjugated in the imperfect: it was being extended, it is being extended, it will have been extended differently when the next event arrives. This is uncomfortable for an industry that wants to ship and forget. It is the condition of taking the human authorship of software seriously. The alternative — the dead-text view of provenance — is not actually more rigorous. It is more brittle, because it cannot survive the events that the living-text view was built to absorb. A hermeneutics of provenance is, in the end, the modest claim that software, because it is made by people and used by people, is subject to the same conditions of interpretation as any other text people produce: that meaning is deferred, retroactive, eventful, and owed. We may as well start reading it that way now, because we will be forced to read it that way anyway, the next time someone we trusted is found to have been somebody else for longer than we knew.
